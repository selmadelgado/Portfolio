About Me

“The Anatomy of a Creative Digital Designer.”

It Started with a Gluestick


Long before responsive design, AI-generated content, and modern web frameworks, I was a kid sitting in art class surrounded by paper, scissors, glue sticks, crayons, and curiosity.

In ninth grade, I signed up for journalism because all the art classes were full. That unexpected detour introduced me to my first Macintosh computer and Adobe PageMaker — and everything changed. Design became more than paper and paste-up boards. It became typography, branding, layout systems, and the early foundation of digital creativity.

That curiosity grew into storytelling, publishing, and visual communication as Editor-in-Chief of the Reagan Statesman newspaper in Houston, Texas. I became fascinated with how words, images, and layout could shape the way people experienced information.

In the early years of my career, I worked night shifts in the graphics department at Kinko’s, designing brochures, business cards, flyers, church bulletins, logos, and print materials long before Canva, social media templates, or drag-and-drop website builders existed. Those years taught me production design, deadlines, detail, and craftsmanship.

While the day and evening crews took the orders, the night crew stayed behind to create them.

I loved the creative process, but what stayed with me most was watching people react to something I had designed. Hearing someone say “wow,” “that’s beautiful,” or “I love it” after handing them a finished piece felt more rewarding than the paycheck itself. Somewhere along the way, I realized that when you genuinely love what you do, it stops feeling like work. I loved it so much that I often volunteered to work the day shift just to keep designing.

That feeling — of creating something that genuinely connects with people — has stayed with me my entire career.

I spent over 13 years at ConocoPhillips as a Business Analyst and web developer supporting enterprise-level applications and the Global Security department.

I taught myself HTML, CSS, and ColdFusion, and was eventually entrusted with building an in-house, mission-critical system to support the safety and security of employees and contractors traveling worldwide on company business. I managed the entire application development lifecycle — and somewhere along the way, the department started calling me the “Resident Genius,” a nod to Steve Wozniak’s role at Apple.

It made the business card.

In 2015, our department was featured in the company’s Spirit magazine, and I was honored to receive two Spirit Awards for my contributions. At that time in my life, it was the best job I’d ever had.

My creative identity didn’t begin with code.
Code became one of the tools.

I later completed the Full Stack Development program through the University of Texas at Austin, expanding my skills in modern development workflows and interactive digital experiences.

The tools have changed constantly over the years.
Newspapers became websites,
print became digital,
marketing became social media,
and now AI is changing everything again.

Yesterday, I attended an invite-only Anthropic Claude workshop in Dallas. I stood under the Gilley’s marquee that read Anthropic Presents — Claude SMB Workshop, met the keynote speaker, and walked the floor with a badge around my neck.

Two badges, twenty years apart.
Same person.
Still learning.
6000pixels is my creative technology studio — a space for building engaging digital experiences for modern businesses and creators. It’s where everything I’ve learned since that kindergarten art table comes together: the craftsmanship of the Kinko’s night shift, the engineering discipline of ConocoPhillips, and now the possibilities of AI.

I still love learning.
I still love designing.
I still love building.

And after all these years, I’m just as curious as the kid sitting in art class surrounded by crayons, paper, and possibility.
