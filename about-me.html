The Anatomy of a Digital Designer.



It Started with a Gluestick

Long before responsive design, AI-generated content, and modern web frameworks, I was a kid sitting in art class surrounded by paper, glue sticks, crayons, and curiosity.

In ninth grade, I signed up for journalism because all the art classes were full. I was introduced my first Macintosh computer and Adobe PageMaker - and everything changed. Design became more than paper and paste-up boards. It became typography, branding, layout systems, and the early foundation of digital creativity.

That curiosity grew into storytelling, publishing, and visual communication as Editor-in-Chief of the Reagan Statesman newspaper in Houston, Texas. I became fascinated with how words, images, and layout could shape the way people experienced information.

The Night Shift at Kinko's

In the early years, I worked the night shift in the graphics department at Kinko’s, designing brochures, business cards, flyers, church bulletins, and logos long before Canva, social media templates, or drag-and-drop website builders existed.

While the day and evening crews took the orders, the night crew created them.

I volunteered to work the day shift — not just to hand clients the finished piece, but to sit with them at the counter, help them choose the right card stock, and offer creative direction upfront on how the flyer, invitation, or layout should look.

Resident Genius

I spent over 13 years at ConocoPhillips as a Business Analyst and web developer supporting enterprise-level applications and the Global Security department.

I taught myself HTML, CSS, and ColdFusion, and was entrusted with building an in-house, mission-critical system to support the safety and security of employees and contractors traveling worldwide on company business. I managed the entire application development lifecycle — and somewhere along the way, the department named me the “Resident Genius,” a nod to Steve Wozniak’s role at Apple.

It made the business card.

In 2015, our department was featured in the company’s Spirit magazine, and I was honored to receive two Spirit Awards for my contributions. At that time in my life, it was the best ever.

My creative identity didn’t begin with code.
Code became one of the tools.

I later completed the Full Stack Development program through the University of Texas at Austin, expanding my skills in modern development workflows and interactive digital experiences.

Still Curious

The medium has evolved over the years. The curiosity never did.
Newspapers became websites,
print became digital,
marketing became social media,
and now AI is reshaping everything again.

I attended an invite-only Anthropic Claude workshop in Dallas. I stood under the Gilley’s marquee that read Anthropic Presents — Claude SMB Workshop, met the keynote speaker, and walked the floor with a badge around my neck.

Two badges, twenty years apart.
Same person.
Still learning.

6000pixels is my creative technology studio — a space for building engaging digital experiences for modern businesses and creatives. It's where everything I've learned since that kindergarten art table comes together: the craftsmanship of the Kinko's night shift, the engineering discipline of ConocoPhillips, and now the possibilities of AI.


I love learning.
I love building.
I love designing.

And after all these years, I’m just as curious as the kid sitting in art class surrounded by crayons, paper, and possibility.
